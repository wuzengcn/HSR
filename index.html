What is health system research

  • It remains a new area in global health
  • It dates back to World Health Report 2000, which defines health system as “all the activities whose primary purpose is to promote, restore or maintain health” (WHO, 2000)
  • It is a multidisciplinary field that draws on economics, sociology, political science, and public health to understand how health systems function and how they can be improved
  • However, there is NO universally accepted definition of health system research, and it can be difficult to define the scope of the field

The role of health financing in health systems

  • Health financing is a critical component of health systems, as it determines how resources are allocated and how services are delivered

    • It includes mechanisms for raising funds, pooling resources, and purchasing services

Why is health system, particularly health financing, research important?

  • In 2010, WHO published a report on health system financing, which highlighted the importance of health financing research in improving health outcomes and achieving universal health coverage (UHC)

  • In 2007, Prof. William Hsiao published a paper 1, calling for better understanding health systems to improve the performance of health systems and achieve UHC.

1 Hsiao, W. C. (2007). Why is a systemic view of health financing necessary? Health Affairs, 26(4), 950-961.

Some key research questions in health system research (examples)

  • How can we design and implement effective health financing mechanisms to improve access to care and reduce financial barriers to care?

  • How can we improve the efficiency and quality of health service delivery?

  • How can we strengthen health information systems to improve decision-making and accountability?

  • How can we improve the governance and leadership of health systems to ensure that they are responsive to the needs of the population?

  • How can we improve the health workforce to ensure that there are enough trained health workers to meet the needs of the population?

  • How can we improve the availability and affordability of essential medicines and technologies?

Methods used in health system research

  • Health system research uses a variety of methods, including:
    • Quantitative methods, such as surveys, administrative data analysis, and econometric modeling
    • Qualitative methods, such as interviews, focus groups, and case studies
    • Mixed methods, which combine quantitative and qualitative approaches to provide a more comprehensive understanding of health systems
  • The choice of method depends on the research question being addressed and the context in which the research is being conducted

Desciplines in health system research

  • Health system research is a multidisciplinary field that draws on a variety of disciplines, including:
    • Economics, which focuses on the allocation of resources and the efficiency of health systems
    • Sociology, which focuses on the social determinants of health and the social dynamics of health systems
    • Political science, which focuses on the governance and leadership of health systems
    • Public health, which focuses on the population health outcomes of health systems
    • Implementation science, which focuses on the translation of research findings into practice
  • The interdisciplinary nature of health system research allows for a more comprehensive understanding of health systems and the complex interactions between different components of health systems

Experiences in Afghanistan Health System Research

  • Afghanistan has a fragile health system that has been severely impacted by decades of conflict and instability

General information and achievement in Afghansitan (2002-2023)

  • General information about Afghanistan (2023)

    • Population: 41.5 million

    • GDP per capita: $413

    • Life expectancy: 66 years

    • Economy has been stagnant before Taliban took over the country in 2021, and droped during COVID-19 pandemic

  • Achievements

    • Life expectancy: 56 (2002) to 66 years (2023)
    • Infant mortality rate: from 104 (2002) to 50 (2023) per 1000 live births
    • Maternal mortality ratio: from 1,263 (2002) to 521 (2023) per 100,000 live births

Current status and challenges in health financing in Afghanistan

  • Not sustainable, and not equitable (NHA 2021)
    • Low level of government spending (3.3%)
    • Donor funding anticipated to decline (19.5%)
    • Substantial share of out of pocket (OOP) spending (77.2%)
    • Low execution of existing government budget for health

Generate evidence for advocacy and informing policy reform in the country

  • Tracking health expenditure
    • National health accounts (NHA)
      • Donor survey; Household survey; Government budget survey
      • Health facility survey
      • Imputation
    • Public Expenditure Tracing Survey
    • Expenditure management Information systems
  • Costing and efficiency analysis
    • Costing of Essential Package of Health Services (EPHS) and Basic Package of Hospital Services (BPHS)
    • Costing of the Naitional Health Strategy 2026-2020
    • Efficiency analysis of district hositals and health facilities

Policy development to guide health reform implementation

  • Health Financing Strategy
  • Revenue Generation Strategy Framework
  • Advocacy plan

Health financing strategy in action (revenue generation and payment)

  • Amended the health law in 2016
    • A turning point in public health history in Afghanistan
    • Paved the way for introducing broader health financing reforms
  • Tobacco tax increased
  • Developed and passed the user fee regulation
  • BPHS/EPHS to transition from contract management to performance management

Stakeholder analysis

  • Objectives: Understand the opinions and attitudes of key stakeholders towards health insurance, and identify potential barriers and facilitators to the implementation of health insurance in Afghanistan

Methods

  • Key informant interviews and focus group discussions
    • Framework
    • Stakeholders
      • High-level government officials from ministries and parliamentarians
      • International donors and organizations (i.e., WHO, the European Commission [EC], USAID, and the World Bank);
      • Private sector agencies (i.e., two private insurance companies).

Key findings

  • The majority of stakeholders were aware of the health financing situation in the country
  • Health financing reform (including the use of health insurance mechanisms) was needed to change the current situation and ensure a more sustainable health financing system for Afghanistan.
  • Leadership and political commitment
    • A majority of stakeholders acknowledged that strong leadership, multisectoral political commitment, and effective collaboration are essential to begin implementing health insurance in Afghanistan

“… political will and political support are very important for establishing health insurance systems in every country. But besides this, for Afghanistan especially, I think it’s more important … because of the constitution, because of the law and the regulations we currently have, especially in terms of having all health services free. So we really need a very strong [political] will and support at different stages and in different entities of the government [to overcome the barriers]”

Results continue …

  • Legal and regulatory environment
    • Stakeholders identified the legal and regulatory conditions for health financing as major challenges that need to be addressed in the short term if a health insurance scheme is to be developed in the future.
  • Quality of health services
    • Quality of care is an important precondition for introducing health insurance

Results continue …

  • Population awareness and trust
    • Many people think that paying money for insurance is worthless. The high illiteracy and poverty rates pose further challenges to educating the population about health insurance
  • Fiscal space, willingness and ability to pay
    • The country’s large informal economy poses a challenge to revenue generation.
  • Capacity to implement and manage a health insurance system
    • There was limited technical and managerial capacity to operate a health insurance system in Afghanistan

Willingness and ability to pay study

  • Objectives: To understand the willingness of the population to pay for health insurance in Afghanistan

  • Methods: A cross-sectional survey of 2,392 individuals in Afghansitan across 8 provinces

  • Survey content: The survey included questions about

    • demographic characteristics,
    • health status, healthcare utilization, and
    • willingness to pay for health insurance

  • Insurance package

    • Health insurance option 1 (I1): Primary and secondary facilities,
    • Health insurance option 2 (I2): I1 + some tertiary healthcare facilities.
    • Medicine option 1 (M1): all prescription medications
    • Medicine option 2 (M2): essential medicines.

Willingness to pay assessment framework and analysis

  • Willingness to pay for health insurance was assessed using a contingent valuation method

Analysis approach

  • Logistic regression for willingness to join health insurance
  • Log Linear regression to examine the factors associated with willingness to pay for health insurance, and the amount of willingness to pay for health insurance

Findings

Willingness to join health insurance

  • 92.4 % and 85.7 % would join the I1 and I2 packages, respectively.

Findings continued (willingness to pay) …

Mean willingness to pay for health insurance was

  • $21.3 for I1; $26.0 for I2
  • $13.4 for M1; $7.4 for M2

Actuarial ananalysis objectives

  • To understand the financial affordability of establishing health insurance schemes in Afghanistan.

Methods

  • Framework

Methods continued

  • Benefit package design
    • Three levels of care: primary care, secondary care, and tertiary care
    • Different copayment rates (0%, 10%, and 20%)
    • Different contribution rates (3% and 6%)
    • Different roll-out strategies (fast roll-out vs. slow roll-out)
  • Cost estimate
    • Unit cost for primary care services in different settings (e.g., mobile health teams, health subcenters, basic health centers, and comprehensive health centers) was estimated using the cost and revenue analysis tool, CORE Plus. A micro-costing tool
    • Unit cost for secondary and thertiary care services
      • mixed methods which combined standard costing of reference services and diagnostic related group (DRG) cost weights.
  • Utilization of care at various levels
    • Service utilization figures were obtained from Afghanistan’s HMIS, largely from the 2018 calendar year

Estimation of contributions and health insurance operational costs

  • Results from willingness to pay study

  • Setup and general operational expenses of the national health insurance authority (NHIA) to manage the Afghanistan health insurance benefit package, and other expenses, such as a reserve fund, administrative expenses, and capital expenditure, are based on interviews

What is happening in Afghanistan now?

  • Health system in Afghanistan is on the brik of collapse
    • NGOs moved out of the country
    • Shortage of medicines and medical supplies
    • Many staff and medical doctors left
  • Donors cannot provide direct support to the government, and have to channel their support through NGOs, which is not sustainable

Key constraints in global health

  • Funding reduced significantly, and the future of funding is uncertain
    • Bilateral aid: 20% decline compared to 2023
    • Multilateral shortfalls: Major institutions like WHO and Global Fund facing significant funding gaps. (e.g., WHO has scaled down its global staff by 25%)
  • Overall global instability, which makes it difficult to generate revenue and implement health reforms in many countries
    • Russia-Ukraine war
    • Gaza-Israel war
    • US-Iran tension
  • Multilaternalism is under threat

Future of global health aid

  • More domestric resources mobilization from LMICs
  • Bilateral support reset
    • US signed $20.2 billion bilateral agreement with 27 countries as of March 2026
    • US shifted from “aid to trade” to partnerships grounded in mutual economic and security interests
  • Tiered contributions
  • Mixed development and humanitarian donors
  • Implementation science remain critical to translate research findings into practice

Key takeaway

  • Health system research is underdeveloped but critical to improving health outcomes and achieving UHC
  • Health system research is a multidisciplinary field that draws on a variety of disciplines to understand how health systems function and how they can be improved
  • Health system research is witnessing substantial changes in the context of global health
  • China has a lot to offer in terms of health system research, and there are many opportunities for collaboration between China and other countries in this field
